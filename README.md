## DASHBOARD 3 - ELECTRICITY PRODUCTION BY ENERGY SOURCE AND RELATED EMISSIONS

- First line and column chart **‚ÄòCoal-Based Electricity Generation and Energy Sector Emissions (1990‚Äì2023)‚Äô**:  
  Electricity generation from coal shows a clear upward trend, especially between 2005 and 2015, when both production and emissions rose in parallel.  
  As coal is a carbon-intensive energy source, the increase in generation directly led to higher greenhouse gas emissions.  
  Although coal-based generation slightly declined after 2020, emission levels remained high ‚Äî indicating the long-lasting environmental impact of coal use.  
  This chart highlights how coal continues to play a dominant yet environmentally costly role in electricity production.  

- Second line and column chart **‚ÄòHydropower Generation and Energy Sector Emissions (1990‚Äì2023)‚Äô**:  
  Hydropower generation exhibits a fluctuating trend, which is natural given its dependence on water availability and seasonal conditions.  
  Despite the variations in generation, emissions have remained relatively stable and low.  
  Even as hydropower production increased after 2010, the corresponding rise in emissions was minimal ‚Äî reflecting the environmentally friendly nature of this renewable source.  
  This chart demonstrates that hydropower contributes positively to sustainable energy production with limited environmental impact.  

- Third line and column chart **‚ÄòRenewable Energy and Waste-Based Electricity Generation and Energy Sector Emissions (1990‚Äì2023)‚Äô**:  
  Since the early 2000s, renewable and waste-based electricity generation has grown sharply, marking a significant shift in the energy mix.  
  Despite this strong increase in production, emissions did not show a steep rise.  
  As the share of renewable sources expanded, the overall rate of emission growth slowed ‚Äî clearly demonstrating the positive effect of the ongoing energy transition.  
  This chart emphasizes the critical role of renewables in achieving sustainable, low-carbon electricity generation.  

- Last line and column chart **‚ÄòNatural Gas-Based Electricity Generation and Energy Sector Emissions (1990‚Äì2023)‚Äô**:  
  Natural gas-based electricity generation increased substantially from 1990 to 2014.  
  Emissions also rose during this period but at a slower pace compared to coal, as natural gas has a lower carbon intensity.

  This chart reflects natural gas‚Äôs transitional role ‚Äî cleaner than coal but still a notable contributor to total emissions.  

### A DETAILED INSIGHT INTO THE 2020-2021 PERIOD:

Between 2020 and 2021, both natural gas‚Äìbased electricity generation and energy sector emissions (shown by the orange line) increased sharply.
When we compare this chart with your other visuals (coal, hydro, and renewable energy), we can interpret this trend more clearly:

- Energy demand rebound after COVID-19:
In 2020, global and national electricity demand dropped due to pandemic restrictions. In 2021, economic recovery led to a strong rebound in electricity consumption, and natural gas plants ‚Äî which are flexible and can quickly ramp up production ‚Äî met much of that increased demand.

- Substitution effect:
During this period, coal-based generation slightly decreased and renewable energy output was volatile due to resource conditions (like water availability).
As a result, natural gas was used more to stabilize electricity supply ‚Äî causing both production and emissions to rise together.

- Carbon intensity correlation:
Natural gas has lower CO‚ÇÇ emissions than coal, but it is still a fossil fuel, so when generation increases, emissions also rise ‚Äî just at a smaller rate per GWh.
The visual confirms this correlation: the rise in GWh (dark blue bars) aligns almost perfectly with the rise in total energy emissions (orange line).

- Energy transition context:
During the 2020‚Äì2021 period, a substitution effect likely occurred, as natural gas partly replaced coal in electricity generation. This shift helped meet the rising post-pandemic energy demand.
After 2021, as renewable energy capacity (especially solar and wind) continued to grow (as seen in your Renewable Energy and Waste chart), the growth of both natural gas generation and emissions slowed ‚Äî showing that renewables started to take over part of the load.

- In summary, the last graph clearly supports the conclusion that as natural gas‚Äìbased electricity generation increases, emissions also increase.
However, the rate of increase is smaller than that of coal, meaning natural gas serves as a transitional fuel ‚Äî reducing emissions compared to coal, but still contributing to total greenhouse gas output.
